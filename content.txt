2) “Our purpose” (homepage section)
Fertility challenges can affect individuals and couples in many different ways—physically, emotionally, socially and financially. Despite progress in reproductive medicine, gaps in evidence and variation in care mean many people still face long waits, unclear diagnoses, limited treatment options, or inconsistent support.
Fertility Research UK exists to help change that by funding high-quality fertility research and helping ensure new knowledge leads to real-world improvements in care and outcomes.


Did you know?
Did you know around 1 in 7 couples experience infertility?
Did you know fertility treatment, including IVF and other assisted reproductive technologies (ART), means there may be at least one child in many classrooms who was conceived through treatment?
While this shows how important fertility treatments are, it’s also important to recognise that we still have limited evidence about the fertility and long-term health outcomes of children conceived through ART—particularly as the oldest IVF-conceived generations reach adulthood.
But what you don’t see are the many people and couples who go through ART without success—often facing repeated cycles, uncertainty, and emotional and financial strain.
Research can change that. Genetic and other biological markers may help predict which assisted reproductive approaches are most likely to succeed, supporting more personalised treatment and helping people avoid ineffective interventions.
That’s why Fertility Research UK exists: to fund research that improves understanding, diagnosis, treatment and care—so more people can get answers, more treatments can work, and support can be better for everyone affected.

About Fertility Research UK
Fertility Research UK is a UK charity focused on supporting fertility research and the people it is intended to help. We believe research is one of the most effective ways to improve fertility outcomes, patient experience and long-term reproductive health.
We are committed to:
Evidence and impact — prioritising high-quality research with clear potential to improve understanding and care.
Lived experience — ensuring research priorities reflect what matters to patients and families.
Supporting research careers — we aim to grow the number of fertility researchers and support their careers by providing funding as well as a supportive network
Collaboration — connecting clinicians, researchers, and communities to accelerate progress.
Equity — supporting work that addresses inequalities in access, outcomes and information.
Our vision
A UK where fertility challenges are better understood, better prevented and better treated—so everyone can access compassionate, evidence-based support and care.
Our mission
To fund, support and champion fertility research across the UK and help translate findings into improved clinical practice, public understanding, and patient experience.

4) What we do (clear headings for a mixed audience)
Fund and support research
We raise funds to support well-designed fertility research projects—from early-stage studies to work that can lead to changes in clinical practice.
Bring people together
We help build partnerships between researchers, clinicians, patients, charities and other stakeholders to reduce duplication and speed up progress.
Promote evidence-based information
We share clear, trustworthy information about fertility research and reproductive health, helping the public and patients understand what is known, what is uncertain, and what research is trying to solve.
Champion better care
We want research to lead to practical improvements: earlier diagnosis, safer and more effective treatment, better guidance, and more consistent support services.
Support the next generation of fertility researchers
A strong research pipeline is essential for progress. We want fertility and reproductive health to be a field where talented people can build sustainable, impactful careers. We support early-career researchers by helping create opportunities, visibility and practical support—so promising ideas and new approaches are not lost.
Grow the UK fertility research community
We work to attract more researchers into fertility and reproductive health by promoting collaboration across disciplines (e.g., genetics, immunology, endocrinology, data science, public health and mental health), and by helping connect researchers with clinicians, patients and research questions that matter.



5) Research focus (stakeholder-friendly, still accessible)
Our current areas of interest (examples)
We support and champion research across fertility and reproductive health, including:
Basic research in reproductive biology: Foundational science that explains how reproduction works (eggs, sperm, fertilisation, embryo development, implantation, placenta and reproductive ageing) and creates the basis for better tests and treatments.
Unexplained infertility and improved diagnostic pathways: Research to uncover hidden causes of infertility and develop clearer, faster routes to diagnosis and appropriate care.
Miscarriage and recurrent pregnancy loss: Studies to understand why miscarriages happen, identify risk factors, and improve prevention, investigation and treatment for those affected.
Endometriosis and PCOS: Research into two common conditions that can affect hormones, ovulation, pain and overall health—aiming for earlier diagnosis and more effective fertility-focused care.
Primary Ovarian Insufficiency (POI): Research into why ovarian function declines earlier than expected and how to improve early detection, fertility options and long-term health support.
Male-factor infertility and sperm health: Work to understand and treat sperm-related infertility, including the impact of genetics, hormones, lifestyle and environmental exposures.
IVF and assisted conception: Research to improve success rates and safety, reduce the physical and emotional burden of treatment, and support smarter, more personalised approaches.
Novel and emerging fertility treatments (e.g., in vitro gametogenesis): Research into new ways of creating or maturing eggs and sperm, and other next-generation approaches, with careful evaluation of effectiveness, safety, ethics and long-term outcomes.
Fertility preservation: Developing and improving ways to protect fertility (for example before cancer treatment) and understanding outcomes for different groups of patients.
Inequalities in access and outcomes across the UK: Research into why fertility care and outcomes vary by geography, income, ethnicity and other factors—and what actions reduce these gaps.
Mental health and wellbeing during fertility investigations and treatment: Evidence on the emotional impact of fertility challenges and what support, counselling and care pathways make the biggest difference.
We will update our priority areas as the evidence base evolves and as we learn from patients, clinicians and researchers.

6) “For patients and families” (supportive but careful wording)
For patients, families and people trying to conceive
If you are experiencing fertility challenges, you are not alone. Many people face uncertainty, delays, or a lack of clear answers. While Fertility Research UK is not a clinical service, we aim to support patients by:
funding research that can improve diagnosis and treatment,
sharing evidence-based information,
amplifying patient priorities to researchers and decision-makers.
If you need medical advice, please speak to your GP or fertility specialist. If you are in urgent distress or crisis, contact NHS 111 (or 999 in an emergency).
